Research and development at Kenana Sugar Company

At Kenana, we've been committed to continuous research and development since the 1970s

Research and development (R&D) is a major contributing factor that helps to increase the yield and quality standard of our sugarcane, providing our customers with unrivalled quality and the freedom to purchase in bulk. We have also expanded our R&D projects to other products we produce, namely ethanol, biogas, compost, poultry, eggs, and fruit.

Applying a comprehensive research programme that addresses the entire scope of our products

Having a holistic R&D programme in place is fundamental to enhancing product quality, meeting local and international demand, and establishing new arms in the business.

Additionally, R&D assists in lowering production costs to drive cost savings for Kenana and our customers while meeting environmentally-friendly standards.

Weed control and growth regulators

Weeds are the most dominant pest that can have a significant negative effect on cane and sugar yields. Our innovative weed science division focuses on eco-biological studies, as well as the study and surveying of weeds and sugarcane flowering. Our objectives include managing weeds and controlling the flowering in cane fields to avoid the adverse effects this can have on cane and sugar yields.

The weed control and growth regulator division of our R&D also conducts studies on cane ripening to boost sugar content in cane at harvest time while also optimising the tons of sugar per unit ton of cane.

Entomology in crop protection

Our entomology division is dedicated to researching pest control including sugarcane borers, termites, and white grubs, all of which are harmful to crops during the growing season.

Our objectives entail monitoring crop pests and assessing any potential infestation by sugarcane pests in the commercial field. This also includes pest control in the produce farm. Additionally, we oversee the prevention of new insect pests via a stringent quarantine procedure.

Furthermore, resistant sugarcane varieties are evaluated, with pesticides being tested for efficacy in pest control. Major pests are controlled through exploring and augmenting natural enemies, and cultural practices are exercised to minimise insect population. Finally, our entomology experts employ hot water treatment to improve the quality of seed cane for optimal production.

Pathology in crop protection

The main objectives of our pathology division in crop protection are to reduce the negative effects of diseases on crop yield and quality through the development of integrated management strategies.

  • The fundamentals of this section involve:
  • Running surveys to monitor diseases for early outbreak warnings
  • Employing stringent quarantine measures to prevent the entry of new diseases
  • Evaluating resistant varieties through the intensive screening of sugarcane diseases
  • Adapting phytosanitary measures to analyse the spread of diseases
  • Producing healthy sugarcane free from disease through the use of thermotherapy and biotechnology

Sugarcane breeding in crop protection

Our crop protection process also entails sugarcane breeding to ensure improvement in varieties. Our key objectives in sugarcane breeding include the vertical increase in sugar yield and reducing the cost of production through advanced innovations and the development of high-yielding cane varieties that are adapted to our specific requirements and standards.

We endeavour to supply sugarcane varieties for particular cultural practices and uses, such as self-defoliators to meet green harvesting or high-fibre varieties for enhanced energy production.

Additionally, variety diversification is a key factor within our sugarcane breeding R&D. This involves careful processes, as the dominance of one variety can increase the risk of disease outbreak. Our sugarcane breeding experts also conduct proper crop planning based on the varying maturity times of different varieties. A number of innovative varieties have been bred and developed by our R&D team which have since significantly contributed to the sustainability of our production.

Crop production: cane nutrition and cultural practices

Known to be an exhaustive crop, sugarcane tends to remove large quantities of nutrients from the soil it grows in, namely macronutrients like magnesium, calcium, nitrogen, and potassium. Thus, providing a sufficient amount of fertiliser to soil and ensuring optimal nutrient management is key.

TOur ongoing research and development approach is rooted in addressing the issue of innovating and optimising fertilisation and cultural practices in the following ways:

  • Monitoring soil fertility to guide fertiliser applications in sugarcane
  • Conducting extensive studies across a number of differential responses to fertiliser
  • Staying up to date with best practices in the use of fertiliser
  • Testing new compound fertilisers
  • Enhancing and conserving the physical and chemical properties of soil for improved plant growth
  • Introducing cost-effective husbandry practices

Soil science techniques in crop production at our soil and plant analysis laboratory

Our state-of-the-art soil and plant analysis laboratory is where we perform a number of tests related to soil science in sugarcane production.

The main functions within our laboratory include providing a premium level of analytical precision, accuracy, and quality across our operations while ensuring timely results pertaining to the testing of soil and plant tissue samples. Our scientists also develop strict testing and analytical techniques for NPK fertilisers which are required for crop and trace elements as part of the criteria for guiding the desired future nutrient applications.

Our laboratory activities include the collection, analysis, and interpretation of soil samples to determine soil fertility limits that have an effect on crop growth, as well as determining the types and quantities of fertilisers to be applied to cultivated fields via laboratory testing.

We perform regular testing on macronutrients, micronutrients, organic matter, electrical conductivity, and the pH levels of soil to ensure optimal soil conditions for sugarcane production.

Our crop monitoring process

There are a number of processes in place when performing crop monitoring for crop production.

We start by studying weather patterns as a key factor that can affect the prospects of crop production. Our crop monitoring section runs the Central Metrological Station of Kenana Sugar Company, assessing the potential impact on sugarcane and other crop through the collection and analysis of weather data.

Field practices are also monitored as we establish cane, growth and ratooning. We closely monitor cane maturity in the field for optimal sugar production by determining the best time for cutting. Finally, our scientists also monitor harvesting practices for maximum sugar output from the field and run sucrose at the laboratory.

Cash crops in crop production

One of our strategies is to diversify our product to ensure it absorbs global sugar pricing.

We conduct comprehensive research on cash crops to identify new crops that are suitable for diversification, particularly including soybean, sesame, sunflower, and other crops. Our research team conducts agronomic and economic studies on cropping systems to drive increased revenue and we also operate a fully certified seed laboratory.

Agriculture engineering for irrigation and water management

To ensure proper irrigation water management, we at Kenana consider two basic factors, namely when and how much irrigation water is to be applied.

Conducting irrigation demonstrations and experiments on a small and large scale are fundamental to irrigation and water management. This enables on-farm irrigation management to improve, while ensuring overall irrigation efficiency, reduced costs, and the monitoring of irrigation practices for the overall estate.

Our irrigation and demonstration experiments are also a crucial factor when determining the gross and nett of irrigation requirements according to the Penman-Monteith approach, the cropwat software for the various seasons, and crop growth stages and cycles.

Horticulture research within our tissue culture laboratory

Our well-equipped tissue culture laboratory is where we conduct research and development using a myriad of techniques to maintain and grow plant cells, tissues or organs under sterile conditions on a nutrient culture medium of known composition.

Our tissue culture laboratory aims to produce a significant number of true-to-type plants free from pests or disease, ensure germplasm conservation, and reduce the lifecycle of plant-breeding programmes.

The list of targeted crops for propagation include:
  • Banana
  • Rose
  • Strawberry
  • Stevia
  • Grapes
  • Sugarcane
  • Pineapple

Animal production: animal nutrition and animal health

Animal Nutrition

Our animal nutrition division is focused on the necessary dietary requirements of animals. Animal feed must be formulated to meet the required nutritional needs that support optimal growth and production. The six classes of nutrients include water, proteins, carbohydrates, fat, vitamins, and minerals.

Our objectives are to improve the milk yield and quality for dairy cattle, performance, and quality of fattening calves, performance and meat quality of broiler poultry, and egg production and quality of layer poultry.

We also strive to reduce the cost of feed manufacturing through the use of more cost-effective sources of feed without compromising on production efficiency.

Animal Health

Whether it involves a single animal or a group, the first and most crucial task when conducting research and diagnosis in animal disease issues remains to ensure accurate diagnosis and research. Surveillance and observation must be recorded within the database, with the two major factors in animal health research including diagnosis, as well as treatment and control measures.

As our animal health division continues to grow in its establishment, we are committed to ensuring that the treatment and control of animal health issues meets the highest standards; this is done through an accurate laboratory diagnosis and the significance of all medical issues. Beyond the research phase, improvement is also a key factor.
